A sustainable lifestyle brand. Its first product is a biodegradable toothbrush, not because the brand is about toothbrushes, but because the toothbrush is one of the most overlooked objects in the daily ritual of waste.

In Korea alone, 200 million toothbrushes are produced each year. None get recycled.

Hero — adult + kids toothbrush in pistachio mint, bathroom counter
The brief, and the reframe

They came for a toothbrush. I gave them a brand.

Econnect came to me to design a PLA toothbrush.

I could have just designed the product. But the material and manufacturing meant the toothbrush would have to sell at a higher price than the bamboo alternatives already on shelf. A higher price needed a reason. Without a brand to give it one, it would land as another eco-friendly product with the same apologetic vibes as everything else.

So I proposed building a brand around a different idea. Choosing sustainability doesn't mean giving up on aesthetics. The product could earn its price by being something people actually wanted to own.

Econnect agreed.

I came on as co-founder, leading creative direction end-to-end.

The problem

The category was apologetic.

Eco-friendly toothbrushes were almost all bamboo, prone to mold in humid bathrooms. They looked like a guilt purchase. Earthy, muted, designed for the people who already cared.

The material

A sustainable object that doesn't announce its sustainability.

The category's problem wasn't really material. It was positioning. But the right material could quietly solve both.

PLA, a corn-based bioplastic, decomposes in 6 to 12 months versus 500+ years for conventional plastic. 70% less carbon in manufacturing. Higher elasticity than conventional plastic, which is actually a functional advantage in toothbrush manufacturing.

And critically, PLA doesn't absorb moisture the way bamboo does. Bamboo's mold problem, gone. The material itself solved the friction that made bamboo feel like a daily compromise.

Product design

Form driven by experience.

Handle angled toward the bristle head for easier reach to back molars. Bristle count increased from 1,000 to 2,500 for a denser, softer brush. Children's version rounded to encourage lighter pressure, designed with how kids actually grip, not against it.

CMF

Pistachio mint, not earth tones.

The color was the most deliberate choice.

Eco products at the time leaned earthy. Muted browns, soft beiges, apologetic greens. HAYAN went the other direction. A pistachio mint that doesn't read as "eco." It reads as home.

A toothbrush sits on the counter, visible every day. Most users hide theirs in cups or drawers because they're not objects worth displaying. HAYAN was designed to be left out. Something you'd be quietly proud to have on the counter.

The pistachio also did commercial work. Adult and kids' toothbrushes shipped in the same color at launch. A household system, not a single SKU. Families bought multiples. Couples bought pairs.

Packaging

Bold message, refined material.

Eco packaging whispered. HAYAN announced itself, I'm not plastic., in oversized type on uncoated kraft. Confidence without loudness.

The system

A product designed to come back.

QR code on the packaging links to a return service. Used toothbrushes get mailed back. Subscription handles replenishment. The brand owns the full lifecycle.
